Welcome To My Website!

Hello, My name is Nichole Badraun and I was diagnosed with Type 1 diabetes at 22 years old. It was such a scary time for me. I had so many new fears, “Am I going to be able to afford the medical care I need?” “Will people stare at my CGM and insulin pump?” “What if I have a low at work?” My future suddenly seemed so uncertain, and my biggest worry was, “Will I be able to have children?” At first I didn’t get a clear answer. My first endocrinologist assured me that, once I got my diabetes under control, a healthy pregnancy would be attainable. My primary care doctor however cautioned against it, saying it would be too risky for my health. The gynecologist I was using at the time terrified me with stories of diabetic women giving birth to giant babies with heart defects. Let me assure you, while it is hard work, pregnancy and a healthy baby are possible with type 1 diabetes. As I slowly adjusted to my new life of carb counting and needles, I started to do more research about Type 1 diabetes and pregnancy. This website is some of that research as well as resources and recipes. Please use what you find useful and disregard what you don’t. I had a relatively uncomplicated pregnancy in 2020 and my beautiful healthy daughter Sloane was born in 2021. I’m in no way an expert, and please consult your doctor before making any changes to your healthcare plan.

So, you are considering starting a family. As a type 1 diabetic it is best to have tight blood sugar control for around 3 months before conception. So, this is a great time to assemble a health team that you are comfortable with! A stellar endo and OBGYN are a must, but you will probably have to meet with a high risk OB at some point. And all of this can be pretty stressful so having access to a mental health professional could be helpful. During preconception another good step would be to make sure your overall health, not just your blood sugar, is in order. And upgrade for tech, if you are considering a CGM or an insulin pump, see if those options are available to you and get into your new routine.

    Notes for your OBGYN

  • Have you had patients with type 1 diabetes before?
  • Do you have a high risk OBGYN that you work with?
  • Can I have a prescription for prenatal vitamins?
  • Am I at a healthy weight for pregnancy

    Notes for your Endo

  • Can you run a blood test to check my thyroid function?
  • What is my A1c?
  • Can we run a urine test on my kidney function?
  • Will you measure my cholesterol?

Congratulations you are pregnant! Your first trimester lasts through the 13th week of pregnancy. During this trimester you might experience morning sickness, fatigue and low blood sugars. As your body begins to grow a tiny human you will require more calories and most likely considerably less insulin than you are used to.

    Notes for your OBGYN

  • Prescription for prenatal vitamins?
  • Talk through the benefits of a NIPT test (usually done around 10 weeks).
  • Talk through foods to avoid and foods that will be beneficial.

    Notes for your Endo

  • Prescription for Glucagon (injection) or Baqsimi (inhalable) for low blood sugars.
  • Check kidney function again.

Recipe - Greek Lemon Orzo Soup with Chicken

INGREDIENTS: 6 cups low-sodium chicken broth, 1/2 cup orzo, 6 large egg yolks, 1 1/2 cups shredded rotisserie chicken, 1/4 cup fresh Meyer lemon juice, kosher salt and black pepper,

INSTRUCTIONS: In a large saucepan set over medium-high heat, bring the broth to a boil. Add the orzo and boil until just tender, about 7 minutes (1 to 2 minutes less than the package directions). In a large bowl, beat the egg yolks until lightened in color. Slowly drizzle in 1 cup of the hot broth, whisking vigorously, until egg mixture is hot to the touch. Take your time, you want to temper the egg yolks here rather than cook them. Add the egg mixture to the saucepan, reduce heat to medium, and cook, whisking occasionally, until thickened to the consistency of heavy cream, about 5 to 7 minutes. Stir in lemon juice. Add the chicken and cook for another minute or two until chicken is heated through. Season with salt and pepper to taste.

First one done, two more to go! The second trimester lasts from week 14 to week 26 of your pregnancy. Some women experience a lessening of symptoms during this time, less nausea, less fatigue. As a diabectic you might find that you are slowly requiring more and more insulin.

    Notes for your OBGYN

  • Ask your OBGYN how long they will allow you to remain pregnant before an induction (Usually diabetics are discouraged to go past 37 week)
  • Start talking to your OBGYN about your birth preferences and your plan for blood sugar management during labor. It’s also a good idea to check with the hospital you plan to deliver at, see if they have any specific regulations about diabetic in labor.
  • Around 20 weeks, ask about checking in with a high risk OB. Usually, they can make sure your baby’s heart is developing properly- a risk with diabetic pregnancies.

    Notes for your Endo

  • Make adjustments to insulin requirements
  • Ask for current A1C (If your insurance will not cover the cost of an A1C test, you can find them over the counter)
  • Check kidney function

WooHoo! You have made it to the final trimester! This trimester you will most likely require a lot more insulin than you are used to. Making sure that you are drinking a ton of water, as well as getting daily exercise will not only help with insulin resistance but also help keep you healthy and strong for your upcoming birth.

    Notes for your OBGYN

  • Your OB will probably have you visit the office much more frequently in your last trimester and they will be closely watching your baby's growth.
  • Your OB will also be keeping a close eye on your blood pressure.
  • Make sure you and your OB go over your birth preferences and are on the same page as far as how your blood sugar will be maintained during labor

    Notes for your Endo

  • As your insulin intake rises make sure you and your endocrinologist make note of your pump settings or insulin intake before pregnancy. After you give birth, you will immediately require much less insulin. Knowing your previous baseline will help you to avoid lows.
  • If you are wanting to manage your own blood sugar during labor, bring this up to your endo. They can help you with a plan for your hospital stay.
  • It's also a good idea to have your most updated a1c.

Items you will need to bring to the hospital

Low carb snacks, Low treatments, Insulin and supplies for about 5 days, Your pre-pregnancy insulin requirements

Why are diabetics discouraged from going past 38 weeks of pregnancy?

Since diabetics can have larger babies, there is a risk of Shoulder Dystocia (when the shoulders get stuck coming through the birth canal). By maintaining good blood sugars throughout pregnancy, this risk is very low.

Are diabetics required to have a C-section?

While this used to be encouraged, as diabetic pregnancies are high risk, a vaginal birth is possible as long as you are progressing normally.

Will the hospital take my baby's blood sugar?

Yes. Because, even with good control, you have more sugar in your blood. This can cause your baby to produce more insulin in the womb. This is easily remedied, but it does need to be monitored.

Recipe - Gazpacho

INGREDIENTS: 1 1/2 pounds vine-ripened tomatoes, peeled, seeded and chopped, Tomato juice, 1 cup cucumber, peeled, seeded and chopped, 1/2 cup chopped red bell pepper, 1/2 cup chopped red onion, 1 small jalapeno, seeded and minced, 1 medium garlic clove, minced, 1/4 cup extra-virgin olive oil, 1 lime, juiced, 2 teaspoons balsamic vinegar, 2 teaspoons Worcestershire sauce, 1/2 teaspoon toasted, ground cumin, 1 teaspoon kosher salt, 1/4 teaspoon freshly ground black pepper, 2 tablespoons fresh basil leaves, chiffonade

INSTRUCTIONS: Fill a 6-quart pot halfway full of water, set over high heat and bring to a boil. Make an X with a paring knife on the bottom of the tomatoes. Drop the tomatoes into the boiling water for 15 seconds, remove and transfer to an ice bath and allow to cool until able to handle, approximately 1 minute. Remove and pat dry. Peel, core and seed the tomatoes. When seeding the tomatoes, place the seeds and pulp into a fine mesh strainer set over a bowl in order to catch the juice. Press as much of the juice through as possible and then add enough bottled tomato juice to bring the total to 1 cup. Place the tomatoes and juice into a large mixing bowl. Add the cucumber, bell pepper, red onion, jalapeno, garlic clove, olive oil, lime juice, balsamic vinegar, Worcestershire, cumin, salt and pepper and stir to combine. Transfer 1 1/2 cups of the mixture to a blender and puree for 15 to 20 seconds on high speed. Return the pureed mixture to the bowl and stir to combine. Cover and chill for 2 hours and up to overnight. Serve with chiffonade of basil.

Congratulations, you are a mama! While having tight blood sugar control isn’t going to be your top priority anymore (because you are trying to keep a very tiny human alive) it's still important to take care of yourself. You can’t give from an empty cup.

    Notes for your OBGYN

  • If you are feeling any depressive symptoms, mention it right away.
  • Take extra care with C-section incisions or if you have had an episiotomy. High blood sugars could delay proper healing.
  • If returning to a birth control method, make sure to discuss the medicanins possible effects on blood sugar.

    Notes for your Lactation Consultant

  • When you meet your lactation consultant for the first time, make sure to let them know that you have type 1. They will have tips and tricks for managing your blood sugar while breastfeeding.
  • Be aware that the herb fenugreek, that is used to promote milk production, can cause low blood sugars.
  • Have your lactation consultant go over the symptoms of a clogged duct, left untreated it could send you into DKA

Are diabetics more likely to experience postpartum depression?

Yes, unfortunately diabetics are 20% more likely to experience postpartum depression

Benefits of breastfeeding for mom

Increased insulin sensitivity

Benefits of breastfeeding for baby

Can reduce the risk for both type 1 and type 2 diabetes

Will I pass diabetes to my baby?

If your partner doesn’t not also have diabetes and you are giving birth after the age of 25, the risk of your baby developing type 1 is 1 in 100.

Recipe - Keto Lactation Cookies

INGREDIENTS: 1/2 cup flaxseed meal, 1/4 cup shredded coconut unsweetened, 1/2 cup almond butter, 2 egg yolks, 1/4 cup coconut oil melted, 1/4 cup butter melted, 2 tbsp erythritol or to taste, 1 tsp vanilla extract, ½ tsp sea salt

INSTRUCTIONS: Preheat the oven to 350 F. In a bowl combine flaxseed meal with shredded coconut. In another bowl beat egg yolks with erythritol, almond butter, melted coconut oil, melted butter, vanilla extract and sea salt. Add flaxseed meal with shredded coconut and mix until smooth. Line a baking tray with parchment paper. Spoon the batter onto the baking tray. Bake 10-12 minutes. Enjoy!
